自由古巴

“Amigo, 你去哈瓦那的行程有托运行李吗？没有的话，我们买一个给古巴人带些物资过去。” Armando在跟我聊质谱之前，总会先聊点别的。

“我回头看看。”

“真的，考虑下。”

我考虑了。但转念想，我真的要干涉别人的生活吗？古巴真的那么匮乏吗？临行前被新老deadline压得喘不过气来，加上经美国去古巴有诸多不确定因素。终究我只是背了个背包。

无序和慢，是这个国家给我的第一印象。

排队入境古巴，我足足站了一个多小时。大厅里挤满了刚下飞机的乘客。没有人组织秩序，有的队列的速度可以是别的队列的五倍快。慢的队伍当然抱怨。后来才知道，如果抱怨，或者抗议有效的话，古巴就不会成为今天这样了。

入住Airbnb后，房东说每天会随机断电大约两三个小时；而哈瓦那以外的城市则是反过来，随机有电两三个小时；使馆区和某些区域是不会断电的。

在第二晚断电的一片漆黑中，Adrian闪闪发光。

Adrian是Airbnb的前台，古巴人，与我同岁，两岁时随父亲在日内瓦呆了两年，后来去了斯洛伐克。教我抽雪茄时真的是一板一眼，煞有介事。我开玩笑说，虽然这是我的第一次，你也别搞得像是要破我处一样。后来断电的几小时中，伴随着朗姆酒，莫吉托，雪茄，听他们聊分手，聊情伤，聊工作中的奇闻，听他对美国和特朗普疯狂输出。

“所有动物都是平等的，只是有的更平等一些罢了。”同理，所有地区都会平等地断电，只是断电的时长不一样罢了。比如，巴拉德罗就会每天稳定断电零小时。两小时车程就可以从哈瓦那到“楚门的世界”，到碧海蓝天，到应有尽有，到灯红酒绿。啥都好，就是假。

全包酒店是国营的，用来招徕游客和外国货币，而员工都是古巴人，领着固定的一个月大约25美金的工资。而酒店外一墙之隔，开复古老爷车的“个体户”就像狼在等溜出猪圈的猪一样，十分钟比墙内员工十天还赚得多。可能他们懂了“造导弹的不如卖茶叶蛋的”的道理，有几十年行医经验的医生也会“弃医从…出租车”，毕竟有个“小家”要养。并不是所有医生都要像六十多年前的那位阿根廷医生一样，或者像太平洋彼岸的孙医生和周医生一样心怀“大家”。

许多年前，古巴人是不许入住巴拉德罗的这些酒店的，大约2008年后，每接待20位外国人，便生成一个古巴人的配额。我打心底里恶心巴拉德罗，不排除部分原因是烈日照出了我内心的阴影和孤独。

“为什么选择来古巴？” Karla问我。

“因为切格瓦拉，他真的帅，不过要声明我不是gay。”我答不上来的时候就喜欢瞎说。我对Che并不了解，我对很多事情都持怀疑态度（除非是我自己手里出的实验数据）；在时光机被发明出来之前，读历史就图一乐。Karla说，在阿根廷的西语里，好友打招呼就称呼“Che”。伟人的一句“切，你好年轻哟”，一语成谶。Che永远活在了39岁。卡斯特罗治下的古巴五十年来碌碌无为，每况愈下。而英年早逝的Che却不担心寿则多辱。反倒是活成了友情，纯粹，理想主义，追逐信仰和个人英雄主义的符号，始终提醒着人们去聆听远方的哭声。

如果没有先去哈瓦那，没有认识Susana，我直飞巴拉德罗的话，应该还是可以当几天“幸福的猪”的。Susana是我的导游，两个多小时给我讲了古巴的历史和社会，带我看了幼儿园，国营的配给物资的小店，私营的商店和药店等等。在幼儿园里参观了孩子们的食堂，Susana给我介绍说，看，这是幼儿园的伙食，一共有四样，但是后两样不存在，因为物资不足。我看看仅有的两样，说到，好像没有蛋白质啊。要么是我英文发音不准，要么是“蛋白质”这个概念对她来说有点陌生，Susana一时没反应过来。我突然懂了什么叫做“何不食肉糜”。

从巴拉德罗再回到哈瓦那之后，或是接受了现状，或是几场暴雨洗却了内心的阴霾，心情不那么沉重了。古巴之行的最后一站定了Viñales Valley一日游。在导游Luis结合自身经历热情洋溢地讲解下，三小时车程眨眼便过了。我们来到Frenchman的烟草农场抽雪茄，听他是如何上交完90%的烟草给政府之后，留25%自己享用并卖给游客。来到Rene和Papita的，咖啡庄园，听老人家的爱情故事。或是因为他们鲜见到中国游客，Rene一个劲儿对我说“chino de china”，还让我去锤咖啡豆。

世界那么大，还有很多别的地方我想去看看，我也不知道我会不会再去古巴。我祝愿Adrian买房或者去了德国，MeMe可以完成纪录片和Mike谈甜甜的恋爱，我会记得Aries，会记得Lili和她的小兔崽子，会记得Ernesto嗯啊嗯啊一个词一个词的蹦英文试图跟我交流，记得和费梅兰同志的友谊。

所以，下一次的旅途，有托运行李吗？你给世界带去什么？

Cuba libre

(Translate by AI. Editted by SL.)

"Amigo, do you have checked luggage for your trip to Havana? If not, we should buy some to bring supplies to the Cuban people." Before discussing mass spectrometry with me, Armando always liked to chat about other things first.

"I'll check on that later."

"Really, consider it."

I did consider it. But then I wondered—should I really interfere with other people's lives? Is Cuba really that deprived? With old and new deadlines crushing me before departure, plus all the uncertainties of traveling to Cuba via the United States, in the end I just packed a backpack.

Disorder and slowness were my first impressions of this country.

Queuing to enter Cuba, I waited standing for over an hour. The hall was packed with people. No one was trying to put order to the chaos, and some lines moved five times faster than others. People in the slow queues would of course complaine. It wouldn’t take long for me to learne that if complaints or protests were useful, Cuba wouldn't be what it is today.

After checking into my Airbnb, the host said the power would randomly cut out for about two to three hours each day; while cities outside Havana had it the other way around—randomly having electricity for two to three hours; embassy districts and certain areas never lost power.

In the pitch-black darkness of a power outage on the second night, Adrian was shining bright.

Adrian was the Airbnb receptionist, a Cuban my age who had spent two years in Geneva with his father when he was two, and later went to Slovakia. When teaching me to smoke cigars, he was really methodical and serious about it. I joked that even though this was my first time, he didn't need to act like he was taking my v-card. Later, during the hours-long blackout, accompanied by rum, mojitos, and cigars, I listened to them talk about breakups, heartbreak, workplace gossip, and heard him passionately rant about America and Trump.

"All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others." Similarly, all areas experience equal power outages, just with different durations. For instance, Varadero consistently has zero hours of power outage daily. A two-hour drive can take you from Havana to "The Truman Show"—to blue seas and skies, to having everything you need, to bright lights and bustling nightlife. Everything's great, except it's all fake.

The all-inclusive hotels are state-owned, used to attract tourists and foreign currency, while the employees are all Cubans earning a fixed salary of about $25 per month. Just beyond the hotel wall, the "business-owners" driving vintage cars wait like wolves for pigs to escape the pen, earning more in ten minutes than the hotel employees make in ten days. Perhaps they understood the principle of "those who make missiles earn less than those who sell tea eggs （造导弹的不如卖茶叶蛋的）"—doctors with decades of medical experience would "abandon medicine for... taxi driving," after all, they have a "small family" who depends on them. Not all doctors need to be like that Argentine doctor from sixty years ago, or like Dr. Sun (Sun Yat-sen 孙逸仙) and Dr. Zhou (Lu Xun 鲁迅) on the other side of the Pacific, who held "the greater family" in their hearts.

Many years ago, Cubans weren't allowed to stay in these Varadero hotels. Around 2008, for every 20 foreign guests hosted, one Cuban quota would be generated. I was genuinely disgusted by Varadero—partly because the scorching sun exposed the shadows and loneliness in my heart.

"Why did you choose to come to Cuba?" Karla asked me.

"Because of Che Guevara—he's really handsome, though I should clarify I'm not gay." I like to talk nonsense when I can't answer properly. I don't really know much about Che; I'm skeptical about many things (except experimental data from my own hands). Until time machines are invented, reading history is just for fun. Karla told me that in Argentine Spanish, friends greet each other by saying "Che." What Mao Zedong said about him "Che, how young you are" became prophetic. Che remained forever at 39. Cuba under Castro's rule has been mediocre and increasingly declining for fifty years. But Che, who died young, doesn't have to worry about the disgrace that comes with longevity （寿则多辱）. Instead, he became a symbol of friendship, purity, idealism, pursuit of faith, and personal heroism, always reminding us to listen to other people crying from afar.

If I hadn't gone to Havana first, hadn't met Susana, and had flown directly to Varadero, I probably could have been a "happy pig" for a few days. Susana was my guide who spent over two hours telling me about Cuba's history and society, showing me kindergartens, state-run rationing stores, private shops and pharmacies, and so on. Visiting the children's cafeteria in the kindergarten, Susana introduced me: "Look, this is the kindergarten's meal plan—there are four items, but the last two don't exist due to insufficient supplies." Looking at the only two items available, I said, "It seems like there's no protein." Either my English pronunciation was off, or the concept of "protein" was somewhat foreign to her—Susana didn't react immediately. I suddenly understood what "Why don't they eat cake? (何不食肉糜)" means.

After returning to Havana from Varadero, whether I had accepted the reality or several downpours had washed away the gloom in my heart, my mood wasn't so heavy anymore. The last stop of my Cuba trip was a day tour to Viñales Valley. Under guide Luis's enthusiastic explanation combined with his personal experiences, the three-hour drive passed in the blink of an eye. We visited Frenchman's tobacco farm to smoke cigars, learning how after surrendering 90% of tobacco to the government, he kept 25% to enjoy and sell to tourists. We came to Rene and Papita's coffee plantation, listening to the old man's love story. Perhaps because they rarely saw Chinese tourists, Rene kept saying "chino (turned out to be a fun Asian stereotype which I won’t consider racist)  de china" to me and had me pound coffee beans.

The world is vast, and there are many other places I want to see. I don't know if I'll go to Cuba again. I wish Adrian can buy a house or make it to Germany, that MeMe can complete her documentary and be nutured in a relationship with Mike. I'll remember Aries, remember Lili and her little brat, remember Ernesto struggling word by word in English trying to communicate with me, remember the friendship with Comrade Karla.

So, for the next trip—will there be checked luggage? And what is in there for the world?
