2025Feb 《Breaking Through: My Life in Science》by Katalin Karikó

传记有啥好读的。我不读传记。上一次读传记还是十年前的乔布斯传。原因很多：不想“小马过河式”地看别人的成功学；无偶像崇拜情结；“听过很多道理，依然过不好这一生”；“正经人谁写日记（此处自己打脸）”，传记同理。

好友去参加mRNA health的会议，给我带回来Kariko的自传。二月提前完成了阅读计划，加上新加坡的长途航班，就不妨带上，万一无聊呢？

于是，我在机场酒吧里读，候机的时候读，在近二十小时的飞机（感谢加航）上读，在酒店读，一口气便过半了Kariko的半生。

其实本来打算继续写“以人为镜”，“好老师”，“好科研”等等话题的。回来之后一直忙，再提笔已经没那股热情了。当真是一鼓作气，再而衰，三而竭。就简单摘抄下吧。希望以后能找Dr. Kariko签名留念。

work and play can bleed into each other, become one and the same, until the very idea of their disyinction feels meaningless

to learn from the world we’be been handed and then try to leave things a little bit better for the next generation

with the right attitude, we can transform negative stress into positive stress. How? By focusing on the things we can control, instead of the things we can’t

Just one more thing. Do your experiments correctly. Change just one variable and ask again.

Wake up at 5:00 AM

Every new finding contributes not only an answer… but also brand-new questions.

Each of those obstacles would be more tangible than contributions I hadn’t yet made.

It always begins as nothing.

I’m not saying don’t correct people. I’m just asking you to do it in a way that’s still encouraging.

Funding pressures can also shape the nature of research.

(on Beijing 2008 Summer Olympic) The opening ceremony was dazzling. I have never seen anything like it in my life, and I’m sure I never will again.

People often fear what they don’t understand

There was so much luck involved. It makes me wonder: Who else was out there and didn’t have such luck? Who could uses a little luck right now? What are we missing?

Teachers, I said, are planting seeds.

No one would ever miss the contribution I didn’t make.

Just keep going with your one more thing, and your one more thing, and your one more thing after that.

What's so good about reading biographies? I don't read biographies. The last time I read a biography was Steve Jobs' biography ten years ago. There are many reasons: I don't want to learn success lessons by 'crossing the river like a little horse' (a Chinese fable about blindly following others' experiences); I have no idol worship complex; 'I've heard many principles, yet still can't live this life well'; 'Who even keeps a diary these days (contradicting myself here)', the same applies to biographies.
A friend attended an mRNA health conference and brought back Kariko's autobiography for me. Having finished my February reading plan early, plus with a long flight to Singapore coming up, I decided to take it along—what if I got bored?
So I read it in the airport bar, while waiting to board, during the nearly twenty-hour flight (thanks Air Canada), and in the hotel. Before I knew it, I'd gone through half of Kariko's life story.
I actually planned to write about topics like 'learning from others,' 'good teachers,' and 'good research.' But after returning, I've been so busy that when I finally picked up my pen again, that initial enthusiasm was gone. It truly is a case of 'the first attempt is full of vigor, the second weakens, and the third exhausts.' I'll just share some simple excerpts. I hope someday I can get Dr. Kariko's signature as a memento.
